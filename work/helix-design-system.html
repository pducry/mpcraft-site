Selected work · 2024

A unified design system across six product teams.

A two-year engagement to consolidate fragmented surfaces into a coherent platform.

Client
Helix
Role
Design lead
Duration
22 months
Team
2 designers, 4 engineers
01 / Brief

Six teams. Three patterns for everything. No agreement on which one was correct.

When I started at Helix, the company had grown by acquisition into a portfolio of six adjacent products. Each one had its own design language, its own component library, and its own implicit theory of what a Helix product should feel like. From the outside, the surfaces looked related — same logo, same general palette — but using two of them in the same workflow was a small act of cognitive whiplash.

The mandate was deliberately vague: "make this feel like one product." There was no roadmap, no committee, and — initially — no buy-in from the individual product teams, who had built their tooling under deadline and were not enthusiastic about anyone arriving to revise it. The first six weeks were almost entirely about listening.

02 / Approach

Start from the contracts, not the components.

The temptation in a project like this is to begin by drawing — to ship a sleek new component library and migrate everyone onto it. We did the opposite. The first deliverable was a written document, about forty pages long, that described what each Helix product was actually for: the user it served, the moment it appeared in their workflow, the few decisions it was uniquely qualified to help them make. We circulated it for two months before drawing a single pixel.

From that document, we extracted a set of cross-product principles — about density, about latency, about how much agency to give the user in each moment — and used those as the load-bearing structure of the system. Components came later, and were treated as a downstream consequence: this is how a button looks because this is what we believe about decisions.

We also made a structural choice that proved important: the system was not owned by a central platform team. It was maintained by a rotating working group of senior designers and engineers from each product, meeting weekly. The system was an institution, not a department.

03 / Outcome

Not a faster team. A team that disagreed about better things.

By month eighteen, every Helix product was running on shared primitives. Time-to-first-pixel for new features had dropped meaningfully. Onboarding time for new designers and engineers had roughly halved. These were the numbers we reported to leadership, and they were real.

But the change I'm proudest of is harder to put on a slide. The arguments inside the company shifted upward. The teams stopped re-litigating button padding and started arguing about whether a particular interaction was honest with the user, whether a default was setting the right expectation, whether a workflow was making a hard decision easier or just hiding it. Those are better arguments. They could only happen because the cheap arguments had been settled.

The system did not remove disagreement. It moved it to where it was useful.
04 / Reflection

What I would do differently.

The mistake I most regret is moving too slowly on tone of voice. We treated copy and microcopy as a phase-two problem, on the theory that visual primitives had to come first. In retrospect, the products felt unified visually months before they felt unified to read, and that gap was confusing for users in a way I had underestimated.

The second thing I would change is the meeting cadence. Weekly was too frequent in the first six months — we were making decisions before the consequences of the previous decisions were visible — and not frequent enough in the last six, when migrations were heaviest. A system's governance, like the system itself, should be allowed to change shape over time.

I left Helix in late 2024 to begin independent work. The system is now maintained by people I trust, and I check in occasionally. So far, it is bending in the right places.
